# Why This Matters

*The file I come back to when the project gets hard and the doubt shows up.*

*Private. Not for the site. For me.*

---

## The question underneath the question

Every long creative project eventually hits the same question: *why am I doing this?*

The project is voluntary. Nobody's paying for it. Nobody asked for it. Nobody will notice if it goes away. The news I'm tracking is mostly not actionable at the individual level. I can't stop the war. I can't change the petrodollar. No vote I cast will register. No policy will shift because of what I write. A rational-utilitarian case for any of this is thin.

So what is it actually for?

## The honest answer

It's not for civic duty. It's not for impact. It's not preparation for action. It's not a scoreboard.

It's for **staying awake inside my own life**, at a moment in history when staying awake is harder and more valuable than usual. The understanding doesn't buy power over events. It buys something smaller and more durable: the ability to be a different person at the dinner table, a different father, a different son, a different brother, a different friend, because of it.

The meaning is located in me, not in the war.

## Prosoche

The Stoics had a word for this: *prosoche*. Usually translated as "attention" or "mindfulness," but more literally something like *holding-toward*. The continuous practice of being aware of the reality you're embedded in, not because awareness gives you power but because *unawareness is its own loss*.

Marcus Aurelius was the most powerful man in the world and most of the *Meditations* are about the fact that even that power didn't let him control events — so the project became staying awake instead. If that was enough for him, it's enough for me.

## The Great-grandpa Larry test

Larry told the craziest, most captivating stories. One day as a kid I heard him tell one about hiking Mt. Everest. My grandma, his daughter, in her sixties, said out loud: *you read that in a book — I read the same book*.

He wasn't lying. He'd read it, the story entered his imagination, the imagination placed him in the scene, and years later the memory of *being there in imagination* was nearly indistinguishable from the memory of *having been there*. The mind does this to everyone at every scale.

The test is not whether you have the story. The test is **what you do when the facts show up**. If Larry laughed and said "caught me" and told it differently from then on, the story was harmless and even good. If he doubled down, it became a lie.

Apply to me. Apply to Jiang. Apply to everyone I read. The willingness to be caught by my grandma is what separates a storyteller from a bullshitter. As long as I keep asking the question, I'm fine.

## Coherence vs. accuracy

Coherence is more satisfying than accuracy to the nervous system. Everyone is running the same hardware. The hardware rewards a new piece snapping into the existing picture with a little hit of *yes, that fits*, and accuracy — which usually leaves you with "I don't know" — gives you almost nothing.

This is why worldviews are sticky. Updating a coherent worldview costs something neurologically that keeping it doesn't. Every piece of contradictory evidence is a tiny pain signal, and the path of least resistance is to explain it away.

The whole editorial posture — symmetric motivations, let the reader conclude, empirical not doomer, skeptical not cynical — is a discipline against my own nervous system. It's uncomfortable on purpose. It's supposed to be.

## Pleasure is not a signal

The inheritance I grew up inside — Lutheran, Protestant work ethic, Weberian seriousness — quietly taught me that if something is enjoyable it can't really count. That *serious* equals *unpleasant* and *pleasant* equals *unserious*.

It's not true. Some of the most serious things a person can do are fun, and some of the most frivolous things are joyless. Feynman called physics *the pleasure of finding things out*. Mary Oliver called attention itself a form of prayer. The Benedictines said *laborare est orare* — to work is to pray — and they meant that the right work, done with absorbed delight, is *already* a form of reverence.

The fact that this project is fun is not evidence it doesn't matter. The fun is the proof the work is alive. Dead work is much worse than live work, even when the dead work is technically more important on paper.

## The test that actually matters

*Does this make me clearer, or does it make me more anxious? Does it help me be present to my life, or does it pull me out of it?*

If the answer is "clearer and more present" — even quietly, even a little — then the project is real and it's doing what it's supposed to do. If the answer is "more agitated and less present," then I've drifted into the failure mode (doom-scrolling dressed as virtue) and I need to step back.

The test is the felt experience, not the content. I'm the only one who can run it, and I have to run it honestly.

## Why it matters for the girls

You are, right now, in the middle of the most important parenting work you will ever do, and almost none of it involves talking to them directly about anything.

The work I'm doing on myself — the reading, the thinking, the writing, the becoming-slightly-clearer-about-the-world — is directly changing the atmosphere of the house they're being formed in. They can already, at some level below their own awareness, feel that dad is engaged with something meaningful, that dad has a practice, that dad cares enough about the world to pay attention to it on purpose. That feeling becomes their background. The background becomes who they are.

The vibe is the whole ballgame. The vibe is downstream of who I actually am. The only way to change the vibe is to change myself. This project is how I'm changing myself. Therefore this project *is* the parenting.

## What I am not trying to be

- Not a news outlet. Not competing with CNN or anyone else.
- Not a platform. Not optimizing for growth or engagement or reach.
- Not a prophet. Not predicting the future. Not telling anyone what to do.
- Not a doomer. The world is hard, but the hardness is not the whole story.
- Not a performance. If nobody reads it, that's fine. It's mostly for me.

## What I am trying to be

- **A dispatch, not a newspaper.** For a person making real decisions in a life they don't fully control.
- **Useful, not urgent.** Clear, not alarmed. Warm, not cold. Dry, not flat.
- **Awake on purpose.** Every day, a little.
- **The kind of father who notices the sky with his daughters and doesn't collapse the wonder into a lesson.**
- **The kind of adult who can be caught by his grandma and laugh and tell the story differently from then on.**
